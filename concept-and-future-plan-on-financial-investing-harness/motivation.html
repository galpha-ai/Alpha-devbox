Motivation

Merging quant systematic investing and 主观 investing

Most discretionary traders have ideas but no research workflow. Most quant platforms have workflow but can't reason about events that have never happened. AlphaDev is the AI researcher that does both — and runs in the background so humans only step in for the high-value decisions.

Backtest any idea. Simulate any scenario.
Automate low-value decisions. Keep humans on high-value judgment.

Why this needs to exist

Discretionary investors have no shortage of opinions. What they lack is a workflow that turns an opinion into something repeatable, verifiable, and falsifiable. Quant systems have exactly that workflow — for the narrow slice of problems that can be cleanly backtested. The slice that can't — post-COVID style shocks, catalyst-driven bets, asymmetric payoffs built on a view of the world — is where discretionary traders spend most of their time, and where they have the fewest tools.

AlphaDev gives discretionary research a quant-grade research harness and gives quant tooling a world-model layer that can reason about events that haven't happened yet. It is not a stock picker. It is a research system.

Two camps that don't talk

Quant systematic

What it's good at

  • Market microstructure, spread, slippage, adverse selection
  • Backtesting, robustness, attribution, failure-mode analysis
  • Shadow mode, sim-to-real gap calibration
  • Fully systematic execution at scale

Where it breaks

  • Events with no historical analog
  • Bets driven by narrative, crowding, or directional views
  • Asymmetric payoff construction from scratch
Discretionary (主观)

What it's good at

  • Fundamentals research and business understanding
  • Reading sentiment, crowding, capital flow, attention flow
  • Constructing asymmetric bets with options
  • Game-theoretic judgment on narrative trades

Where it breaks

  • No consistent way to turn a view into a forecast
  • Hard to tell if a win was process or luck
  • Hard to compound research across a team

Three kinds of money in a stock

An old trader's way of seeing the market. A serious AI researcher has to be able to reason about all three.

1. Fundamentals money

You make money because the business improves or deteriorates, and the market slowly moves toward that truth.

The most objective. Closest to what you can actually investigate. Buffett's dog — not the leash.

2. Trend money

Crowding. Everyone decides the same thing is good, piles in together, and momentum feeds on itself.

Until someone trims. Momentum crashes come from the inside of the crowd, not the outside.

3. Game money

You sell the story; someone else carries the bag. Recursive belief chains: I think that you think that I think.

Hardest to play. Ends with a cliff-jumping game the moment belief cracks.

Two classes of research problem

Class A

Forecast & Backtest

There is a target and enough history. Turn the view into ŷₜ, run a rolling predictive model, compare to realised y, produce standard statistical properties.

  • Rolling linear / tree / DNN models
  • Trigger-based strategies (enter / exit conditions)
  • Triggers can be price, volume, earnings, news, a tweet, or the output of a previous model
Class B

Scenario & Simulation

No clean historical analog. Run bear / base / bull. Size by Kelly. Use multi-agent simulation to play out how the world unfolds. Calibrate against real prediction markets.

  • Construct asymmetric payoff — structure first, probability second
  • Build what-if simulations, replay against reality after the event
  • Use markets like Polymarket as an external probability signal

What's really missing — a Financial World Model

In traditional quant, "the world model" is actually a market impact model: spread, fill rate, adverse selection. That is useful but local. It tells you about execution cost, not about what is going to happen.

The harder object is a model of the causal relations between events — earnings, macro prints, geopolitical catalysts, and sector narratives. Something that can reason about a scenario that hasn't yet happened and expose the right compute to the user. That's the multi-year research program AlphaDev sits on top of. Year one lays the scaffolding for it.

Many big discretionary bets are not alpha that would work if market impact were zero. They are risk-limited, not market-impact-limited. What you earn is a risk premium for a scenario bet. — AlphaDev handoff notes, U4

Five worked examples

Each example is the same shape: a trader types something in natural language, the AI researcher decides which layer to use (backtest vs scenario vs operator), runs the research, and returns a report.

Class A · Backtest

Example 1 — "Do earnings surprises keep working after a gap up?"

backtest: buy SP500 names gapping up >3% post-earnings, hold 5 days, 2015–today. show sharpe, turnover, and max drawdown. split by sector.
STEP 1
Pull earnings dates + post-open gaps from ClickHouse. Split train / test by year.
STEP 2
Rolling walk-forward: position = long $1 per qualified name, 5-day hold.
STEP 3
Return Sharpe, turnover, DD, per-sector attribution, failure-mode table.

What the user would never write by hand: point-in-time universe, survivorship filter, clean sector mapping. The harness handles it.

Class B · Scenario

Example 2 — "If the Fed cuts 50bp in July, who wins?"

scenario: fed cuts 50bp at the july meeting. give me bear / base / bull on long-duration software and on regional banks. size with kelly. calibrate probability against polymarket.
Bear
−18%
No cut, hawkish dots. Software compresses.
Base
+9%
50bp cut, neutral guidance. Repricing in line.
Bull
+28%
Cut + dovish path. Duration bid, regionals re-rate.

Kelly sizing comes from the mapped payoff × probability. Probabilities are anchored by current Polymarket pricing on the Fed path, not pulled from thin air.

Class B · Asymmetric

Example 3 — "Build me a cheap asymmetric bet on the AI capex cycle."

construct an options structure on NVDA-complex names that loses <30% in a crash and makes 3–5× in a continued capex ramp. keep cost budget under 1% of notional per 3 months.
STEP 1
Pick the names with the cleanest capex beta. Pull listed options term structure.
STEP 2
Builder tries call spreads, risk reversals, and calendars. Optimises for payoff asymmetry at fixed cost.
STEP 3
Return best structure, greeks, scenario payoffs, and what breaks it.

The point is structure first, probability second — the asymmetry is built into the payoff, not estimated from a point forecast.

Three kinds of money · Trend / Game

Example 4 — "Is this stock still a trend trade or has it become a game trade?"

run a crowding check on XYZ. who owns it, how long-only vs hedge fund, what does the news flow look like in the last 30 days, are we still in the momentum phase or starting to see distribution?
STEP 1
Holdings + ownership diffs. Classify holders into types.
STEP 2
News and social volume by day. Sentiment regime shift detection.
STEP 3
Multi-agent sim: how does the crowd react if one large holder trims? Is this momentum, distribution, or a narrative-handoff game?

Same hosted research session a team can argue over in Slack — same facts, same numbers.

Layer C · Operator

Example 5 — "Put the idea live at small size and tell me when reality disagrees."

promote the gap-up strategy from example 1 into shadow mode for two weeks, then live at $500 per signal. alert me if realised fills, impact, or reason-for-move start to diverge from the sim.
STEP 1
Shadow run. Sim fills, spreads, market impact logged per trade.
STEP 2
Small-live run, capped at $500 per signal. Every live fill compared against sim.
STEP 3
Process validation: for each closed trade, did the stock move for the reason the strategy assumed? If not, raise a review in the Slack thread.

Below $1,000: automated. Above $1,000: human approval. Low-value decisions to the machine, high-value decisions to the person.

Validation — process, not just PnL

Markets are noisy. A good process can lose and a bad process can win. Grading only on realised PnL is slow and misleading.

Outcome axis

Trajectory-averaged PnL across a cohort of trades. The usual.

Process axis

Did the stock move for the reason we predicted? Did the causal chain we wrote down actually fire? Did fundamentals, sentiment, and flow evolve in the direction the thesis implied?

Grading both axes gives usable feedback in weeks instead of years. The process check is a first-class feature, not an afterthought.
